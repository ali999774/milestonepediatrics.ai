Key insights — how this differs from a traditional org chart
Dev Kumar outranks Elena — but not on paper
Dev Kumar earns less and has a junior title. But he holds absolute blocking power over Elena — the highest-paid non-lead. This is gate authority: you don't need a senior title to hold a gate. In real firms, QA engineers who understand this dynamic become politically important.
Marco has authority without a report line
Priya and Tyler don't report to Marco — they report to Jordan. But they cannot override Marco's design tokens. His authority is enforced through the code itself, not through management. Real designers who understand this build systems that are self-enforcing.
Rachel's contract authority is invisible until integration
Rachel works in isolation and never talks to Priya or Tyler directly. But the TypeScript interfaces she publishes are law — Tyler must build his form to match them, and Jordan must wire them correctly. If Rachel's types are wrong, integration fails silently.
Sophia coordinates everything but controls nothing
Sophia has no technical veto. She cannot override Marco's color choices or Rachel's schema. Her authority is purely coordinative — she manages the process, not the people. This is why PMs who try to make technical decisions lose credibility fast in real firms.
Jordan is the only person who holds authority twice
Act 1: technical authority as scaffold architect — sets the foundation everyone builds on. Act 2: integration authority — the only person with a full view of all parallel work, with the power to accept or reject what everyone produced. This double role is why Jordan is the highest-paid engineer and why Act 2 uses the most powerful model. In a real firm, this person is often called a "Staff Engineer" or "Principal Engineer" rather than just a lead.
